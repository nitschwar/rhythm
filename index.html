Relating your brain to your cycle may seem unfamiliar — but the evidence is clear. Estrogen and progesterone cross the blood-brain barrier and directly shape mood, memory, motivation, and effort perception.1,2 Rhythm translates that science into daily, personalized behavioral interventions.

The core idea

Your brain and your cycle are in constant dialogue.

Have you ever had an incredibly stressful week, and then gotten sick right after? Researchers call this neuroimmunology — the same principle that connects mental health to physical health. Your menstrual cycle is no different. Reproductive changes impact your brain, and your brain impacts reproductive changes.

Research also shows that between-person variation — who you are — often exceeds between-phase differences.6 Phase gives context. You provide the data. Rhythm uses both.

Menstrual

Estrogen and progesterone at nadir. Effort perception elevated. Low-intensity interventions prioritized.

Follicular

Rising estrogen upregulates BDNF and serotonin sensitivity. Verbal memory strongest. Initiation easier.

Luteal

Progesterone dominant. HRV decreases. Frustration tolerance lower. Autonomic regulation prioritized.

But —

Your daily check-in overrides phase defaults. Because you are not an average.

Across the lifespan

Rhythm works across every reproductive stage.

The cycle

Estrogen and progesterone cycle across ~28 days, modulating serotonin, GABA, dopamine, and BDNF systems.1 These are not mood swings. They are measurable neurobiological states.

Perimenopause

When cycles become irregular, estrogen fluctuations become unpredictable. Sleep, mood, and cognition follow. This is a neurological transition — not just a reproductive one.7

Postpartum

Within 48 hours of birth, estrogen falls faster than at any other point in the human lifespan.8 Cycles return irregularly. The irregularity is normal. You will find your rhythm again.

From the research

Five things the evidence actually says.

Cognition

Verbal memory peaks in the follicular phase — and it's not subtle.

The same neurobiological systems that drive your mood also drive your working memory, processing speed, and attention. Scheduling around this is not optimization theater — it's applied neuroscience.3

Sleep

Your sleep changes across your cycle. And bad sleep dysregulates the same axis that controls it.

Progesterone raises core body temperature in the luteal phase, suppressing deep sleep. HRV decreases. Mood the next day reflects this — even when you don't consciously register poor sleep quality.9

Stress

Chronic stress suppresses ovulation. The HPA axis and the reproductive axis share infrastructure.

This is not metaphorical. Cortisol directly inhibits GnRH pulsatility. The same behavioral interventions that lower cortisol have documented effects on cycle regularity.4

Postpartum

The postpartum period is the most acute hormonal shift in the human lifespan. The brain is prepared for it — but not in the way most people expect.

Within 48 hours of birth, estrogen falls faster than at any other point in your life. This is a feature, not a failure. But knowing what to expect neurobiologically changes how you interpret what you are experiencing.8

Effort

We consistently overestimate how hard things will feel before we start. This bias is measurably larger in the luteal phase.

Effort perception — the anticipated cost of action — is a neurobiological construct, not a personality trait. It is modulated by hormonal state, autonomic arousal, and prior experience. Knowing this changes how you make decisions when things feel hard.12

The app

Personalization at the intersection of cycle science and mood.

Rhythm is not a symptom tracker. It is an intervention delivery system, personalized by where you are in your cycle, how you feel today, and who you are as a psychological individual.

How it works

Three layers of personalization. One coherent recommendation.

Onboarding

Who You Are

Before Rhythm recommends anything, it builds your profile. This includes:

  • Age — to contextualise cycle stage and neurobiological baseline
  • Cycle regularity — regular, somewhat irregular, or highly variable
  • Average cycle length — used to estimate current phase
  • Perimenopause indicators — for women 40+ or with irregular cycles, Rhythm activates an adapted interpretation model
  • Recent birth — within the past 12 months triggers postpartum mode; breastfeeding status is recorded as it affects cycle return timeline and hormonal suppression
  • Hormonal contraception or HRT — which changes the phase-inference logic entirely
  • Relevant diagnoses — PCOS, endometriosis, thyroid conditions
  • Psychological traits — anxiety sensitivity, emotion regulation style, sleep chronotype, mindfulness background
Layer 01

Cycle Phase Context

Your current menstrual phase (menstrual, follicular, ovulatory, or luteal) provides the neuropsychological backdrop. For women in irregular or perimenopausal cycles, this layer is weighted lower — daily mood data takes over as the primary signal.

Layer 02

Daily Mood Calibration

A brief daily check-in captures your actual state: energy level, emotional valence, cognitive load, and physical symptoms. This real-time layer overrides phase defaults — because you know yourself better than a population average does.

What rhythm does

From data to action, every day.

Each day, Rhythm combines your phase context, daily check-in, and trait profile into one ranked recommendation — with a two-sentence explanation of why, drawn from the research literature.

Each intervention takes 5–10 minutes. Each one is linked to a full article in The Science. Each one ends with a brief check-in.

Design principle

Understanding your own neuropsychology is the first intervention. Everything else follows from that.

Why this matters

The gap is not a cycle tracker.
It's an intervention layer.

Cycle trackers give you data. Wellness apps give you generic interventions. Neither connects the two. Rhythm does.

A breathing exercise is not the same intervention for a woman in her luteal phase with high anxiety sensitivity as it is for a woman mid-follicular who runs every morning. Context matters. Rhythm builds it in.

This is what personalized behavioral medicine looks like.

Intervention library

What Rhythm recommends — and why.

Breathwork

Respiratory-paced breathing modulates vagal tone and HPA axis reactivity. Particularly effective during the luteal phase and in high-anxiety states.

RCT-supported

Meditation

Mindfulness-based practices reduce amygdala reactivity and cortisol output. Effect sizes are strongest for trait-anxious individuals and premenstrual symptom clusters.

Meta-analysis supported

CBT

Cognitive restructuring and behavioral activation are the most evidence-dense interventions available for mood dysregulation, effective across cycle phases but especially during luteal and perimenstrual windows.

Gold standard

ACT

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy reduces psychological inflexibility and experiential avoidance — particularly relevant for women with PMDD or cycle-related mood disruption who benefit from defusing from difficult internal states.

RCT-supported

Movement

Aerobic exercise is anxiolytic and antidepressant, with neuroplasticity effects via BDNF upregulation. Rhythm adjusts intensity recommendations based on cycle phase — high-intensity during follicular, restorative during late luteal.

Systematic review

Journaling

Expressive writing and structured self-monitoring reduce rumination and improve emotional processing. Rhythm offers phase-specific prompts that draw from IFS, CBT, and narrative therapy frameworks.

Evidence-based

Sleep Hygiene

Sleep architecture shifts measurably across cycle phases. Rhythm provides personalized sleep hygiene recommendations that account for progesterone-driven thermogenic effects and luteal-phase REM disruption.

Clinical guideline-aligned

The research

Built on original psychophysiology research.

Rhythm is grounded in original graduate research on the psychophysiology of effort perception and subjective experience across the menstrual cycle, using ecological momentary assessment (EMA) in a naturalistic longitudinal design. The research examines menstrual phase effects on mental and physical effort, with heart rate variability (HRV) as a psychophysiological moderator.

This work was conducted as a graduate thesis in collaboration with researchers at Tel Aviv University, the University of California Santa Barbara, and Cambridge — spanning reproductive neuroendocrinology, psychophysiology, and behavioral measurement methodology.

The research directly informs Rhythm's core design premise: that within-person variation in effort, mood, and cognitive experience is substantial, phase-sensitive, and insufficiently addressed by existing behavioral health tools.

Mission

Evidence-based.
Individual-first.
Not pink and fluffy.

Women's health has a wellness problem. The market for cycle apps, hormonal supplements, and "femtech" is growing rapidly — but the signal-to-noise ratio is low. Aesthetic branding has outpaced clinical evidence. Products are marketed on social media virality rather than randomized controlled trial data. The dominant palette is pink. The dominant claim is empowerment, undefined.

Rhythm is a deliberate alternative. Every feature is grounded in peer-reviewed literature. Every intervention is selected based on effect size and population relevance. The design is clean because the content is rigorous. This is not a wellness brand. It is a clinical decision-support framework for individual neuropsychological health, delivered at consumer scale.

The goal is not to make women feel good about their cycle. The goal is to give them the tools to understand their own neuroscience and act on it — intelligently, consistently, and without mystification.

For employers and collaborators

A portfolio project in end-to-end digital health product thinking.

Rhythm demonstrates the full arc of evidence-based digital health product development — from clinical literature synthesis and research translation to product architecture and AI-powered personalization logic. It is built to the standard of a real product, by a researcher who has worked at the intersection of reproductive neuroscience, women's health policy, and clinical outcomes.

  • Product management: end-to-end product specification — user need identification, evidence synthesis, feature logic design, intervention taxonomy, onboarding flow, and personalization architecture
  • Innovation management: identifying a validated gap between consumer cycle apps (data only) and wellness apps (intervention, no biological context) and designing the product layer that bridges them
  • Therapeutic program design: mapping intervention types (CBT, ACT, breathwork, movement, sleep hygiene) to clinical evidence, cycle-phase neuropsychology, and individual trait profiles — the same logic that underpins digital therapeutic program development
  • Clinical evidence translation: converting peer-reviewed neuroscience and psychophysiology into accessible product copy, UX decisions, and recommendation logic — without losing scientific precision
  • Research foundation: original graduate thesis on reproductive neuropsychology in collaboration with researchers at Tel Aviv University, UC Santa Barbara, and Cambridge; published work in the Lancet Group, International Journal of Cancer, and BMC Public Health
  • Patient-centered outcomes thinking: ecological momentary assessment and PRO methodology directly informs both the thesis research design and Rhythm's daily check-in architecture
  • Stakeholder communication: demonstrated ability to translate complex neuroendocrinology for non-specialist audiences — across this site, published work, and science communication practice

The roles this work is most directly relevant to: Product Manager, Innovation Manager, and Therapeutic Program Manager in digital health, femtech, medtech, or life sciences.
