Perimenopause

When your cycle becomes less predictable — your brain doesn't have to be.

Perimenopause — the transition that begins, on average, four to six years before the final menstrual period — is not a single event. It is a prolonged period of hormonal variability: estrogen levels that fluctuate unpredictably, cycles that lengthen and shorten, ovulation that becomes irregular. For many women, it starts in their early-to-mid 40s. For some, earlier.

The neuropsychological effects — disrupted sleep, altered mood regulation, cognitive changes, shifts in anxiety threshold — are real, measurable, and often unsupported by existing health tools. Rhythm is built to support women across the full reproductive lifespan, including this transition.

What changes

Cycle length and regularity

Cycles may shorten, lengthen, or become anovulatory — sometimes within the same few months. The predictable four-phase rhythm becomes a probability distribution. Rhythm adjusts its phase interpretation model accordingly, using your reported cycle data rather than a fixed-length assumption.

What this means neurologically

Greater hormonal variability, more neuropsychological noise

Erratic estrogen fluctuations — rather than steady decline — are associated with mood instability, sleep disruption, and cognitive symptoms. The brain regions most sensitive to estrogen (hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, amygdala) are the same regions underlying memory, executive function, and emotion regulation.

What Rhythm does differently

Irregular cycle mode + perimenopausal intervention stack

When onboarding indicates age 40+ and/or cycle irregularity, Rhythm activates a perimenopausal mode: heavier weighting on daily mood data over phase predictions, sleep hygiene prioritization, CBT-based approaches for mood lability, and autonomic regulation via breathwork. The intervention logic adapts to the less predictable hormonal terrain.

Pregnancy & the fourth trimester

Your cycle stops.
Your brain doesn't.

Pregnancy silences the menstrual cycle — but neurobiologically, it is one of the most active periods of a woman's life. Estrogen and progesterone reach concentrations orders of magnitude above their cycle peaks. Oxytocin, prolactin, and relaxin reshape both body and brain. And the postpartum window — the so-called fourth trimester — involves one of the most precipitous hormonal withdrawals in human biology.

Rhythm is built to support women through this transition: from the moment cycles return after birth, through the weeks and months of irregular, anovulatory, breastfeeding-modulated cycles, back to a stable rhythm. The research on this window is still developing — but what we do know is clinically significant and largely unaddressed by existing tools.

During pregnancy

The brain remodels — structurally

Grey matter volume changes during pregnancy are detectable by MRI and persist for at least two years postpartum (Hoekzema et al., 2017). These are not simply hormonal fluctuations — they represent enduring neuroplasticity, likely related to social cognition and maternal attunement. Progesterone and estrogen at pregnancy concentrations also alter GABAergic tone, sleep architecture, and emotional reactivity in ways that are distinct from the cycle.

The postpartum drop

Estrogen falls faster than at any other point in the lifespan

Within 24–48 hours of delivery, estrogen and progesterone plummet from their pregnancy peaks to below pre-pregnancy levels. This is the fastest hormonal withdrawal in human biology — and it is the leading neurobiological hypothesis for postpartum mood disorders. Prolactin simultaneously rises, suppressing GnRH and delaying cycle return. The brain must recalibrate across multiple systems simultaneously.

Clinical signpost

Postpartum mental health is a clinical domain

Rhythm supports the postpartum neuropsychological transition — but postpartum depression, anxiety, and psychosis require clinical care. If you are experiencing persistent low mood, intrusive thoughts, or emotional symptoms beyond the first two weeks postpartum, please reach out to a specialist.

When does your cycle return?

The honest answer: it varies — and the research is still incomplete.

Without breastfeeding, menstruation typically returns within 6–8 weeks postpartum. With exclusive breastfeeding, prolactin suppresses ovulation — the lactational amenorrhea mechanism — and cycle return may be delayed from a few months to well over a year. But this is probabilistic, not guaranteed. Ovulation can precede the first postpartum period, meaning pregnancy is possible before the first bleed returns. The exact timeline and its neuropsychological correlates remain an underresearched area.

What those first cycles are like

Irregular, often anovulatory, and neuropsychologically noisy.

The first postpartum cycles frequently lack ovulation — the hormonal machinery is restarting, not yet running at full capacity. Cycle length may be erratic. Luteal phases may be shorter than baseline. The hormonal profiles of these early cycles differ from pre-pregnancy norms, often with lower progesterone output and less predictable estrogen peaks. Mood, energy, and cognitive load during this period can feel unstable in ways that don't map cleanly onto the cycle phase model you may have known before.

How Rhythm supports this window

Postpartum mode — then, gradually, back to regular Rhythm.

When onboarding indicates a recent birth (within 12 months), Rhythm activates postpartum mode: phase prediction is suspended, daily mood data becomes the primary signal, and interventions are calibrated for sleep deprivation, emotional volatility, and autonomic dysregulation. Breastfeeding status is factored into the cycle-return model. As cycles regularise and you report more consistent patterns, Rhythm transitions you — gently, gradually — back to the full phase-aware model. The irregularity is normal. It won't last. You'll find your rhythm again.

Conception & pregnancy

The pregnant brain is not a paused brain.
It is an actively remodelling one.

Structural neuroplasticity during pregnancy is now documented by neuroimaging research (Hoekzema et al., 2017; Carmona et al., 2019). Grey matter volume changes — concentrated in regions associated with social cognition, theory of mind, and self-referential processing — are detectable from the first trimester and persist years beyond birth. These are not deficits; they appear to represent adaptive specialisation for caregiving demands.

First-trimester progesterone and hCG contribute to fatigue, nausea, and what is often called "pregnancy brain" — cognitive changes including slowed processing speed and mild memory effects that are real and measurable, though frequently overstated in popular accounts. Second-trimester estrogen peaks bring a window of relative stability. Third-trimester progesterone dominance modulates sleep architecture and increases emotional reactivity as the brain prepares for birth and bonding.

This section of Rhythm is in active development. The science of the pregnant brain is expanding rapidly — and it deserves serious attention in a women's neuropsychological health tool.

"Pregnancy is associated with a decrease in global grey matter volume that is selective, long-lasting, and — importantly — not associated with cognitive decline. It is adaptation, not deterioration."

The app

Personalization at the intersection of cycle science and mood.

Rhythm is not a symptom tracker. It is an intervention delivery system, personalized by where you are in your cycle, how you feel today, and who you are as a psychological individual.

How it works

Three layers of personalization. One coherent recommendation.

Onboarding

Who You Are

Before Rhythm recommends anything, it builds your profile. This includes:

  • Age — to contextualise cycle stage and neurobiological baseline
  • Cycle regularity — regular, somewhat irregular, or highly variable
  • Average cycle length — used to estimate current phase
  • Perimenopause indicators — for women 40+ or with irregular cycles, Rhythm activates an adapted interpretation model
  • Recent birth — within the past 12 months triggers postpartum mode; breastfeeding status is recorded as it affects cycle return timeline and hormonal suppression
  • Hormonal contraception or HRT — which changes the phase-inference logic entirely
  • Relevant diagnoses — PCOS, endometriosis, thyroid conditions
  • Psychological traits — anxiety sensitivity, emotion regulation style, sleep chronotype, mindfulness background
Layer 01

Cycle Phase Context

Your current menstrual phase (menstrual, follicular, ovulatory, or luteal) provides the neuropsychological backdrop. For women in irregular or perimenopausal cycles, this layer is weighted lower — daily mood data takes over as the primary signal.

Layer 02

Daily Mood Calibration

A brief daily check-in captures your actual state: energy level, emotional valence, cognitive load, and physical symptoms. This real-time layer overrides phase defaults — because you know yourself better than a population average does.

What rhythm does

From data to action, every day.

Rhythm synthesizes your phase context, daily check-in, and baseline trait profile to generate a ranked set of evidence-based interventions — with an explanation of why each is recommended, drawn from the underlying research literature.

Unlike apps that deliver the same meditation to every woman on day 14 of her cycle, Rhythm understands that a high-anxiety woman in her luteal phase who slept poorly needs a different intervention than a low-anxiety woman in her follicular phase who runs regularly.

Each intervention comes with: a 5–10 minute guided exercise, the evidence base summarized in two sentences, a link to the relevant article in The Science, and a check-in after completion.

Design principle

Understanding your own neuropsychology is the first intervention. Everything else follows from that.

Why this matters

The gap in the market is not a cycle tracker.
It's an intervention layer.

Cycle tracking apps — from Clue to Flo to Natural Cycles — have built large audiences by giving women data about their cycles. That is genuinely valuable. But data without action is surveillance, not health. None of these apps has built a clinically-grounded, psychologically-personalized intervention delivery system on top of cycle phase information.

Meanwhile, general-purpose mental wellness apps — Calm, Headspace, Woebot — provide evidence-based interventions with no understanding of the neuroendocrine context in which those interventions are delivered. A guided meditation is not the same intervention for a woman in the late luteal phase with high anxiety sensitivity as it is for a woman mid-follicular with a strong mindfulness background.

Rhythm is the bridge: cycle phase awareness + individual psychological profiling + evidence-based behavioral intervention, unified into a daily practice that adapts in real time. This is what personalized medicine looks like at the behavioral level.

Intervention library

What Rhythm recommends — and why.

Breathwork

Respiratory-paced breathing modulates vagal tone and HPA axis reactivity. Particularly effective during the luteal phase and in high-anxiety states.

RCT-supported

Meditation

Mindfulness-based practices reduce amygdala reactivity and cortisol output. Effect sizes are strongest for trait-anxious individuals and premenstrual symptom clusters.

Meta-analysis supported

CBT

Cognitive restructuring and behavioral activation are the most evidence-dense interventions available for mood dysregulation, effective across cycle phases but especially during luteal and perimenstrual windows.

Gold standard

ACT

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy reduces psychological inflexibility and experiential avoidance — particularly relevant for women with PMDD or cycle-related mood disruption who benefit from defusing from difficult internal states.

RCT-supported

Movement

Aerobic exercise is anxiolytic and antidepressant, with neuroplasticity effects via BDNF upregulation. Rhythm adjusts intensity recommendations based on cycle phase — high-intensity during follicular, restorative during late luteal.

Systematic review

Journaling

Expressive writing and structured self-monitoring reduce rumination and improve emotional processing. Rhythm offers phase-specific prompts that draw from IFS, CBT, and narrative therapy frameworks.

Evidence-based

Sleep Hygiene

Sleep architecture shifts measurably across cycle phases. Rhythm provides personalized sleep hygiene recommendations that account for progesterone-driven thermogenic effects and luteal-phase REM disruption.

Clinical guideline-aligned

The research

Built on original psychophysiology research.

Rhythm is grounded in original graduate research on the psychophysiology of effort perception and subjective experience across the menstrual cycle, using ecological momentary assessment (EMA) in a naturalistic longitudinal design. The research examines menstrual phase effects on mental and physical effort, with heart rate variability (HRV) as a psychophysiological moderator.

This work was conducted as a graduate thesis in collaboration with researchers at Tel Aviv University, the University of California Santa Barbara, and Cambridge — spanning reproductive neuroendocrinology, psychophysiology, and behavioral measurement methodology.

The research directly informs Rhythm's core design premise: that within-person variation in effort, mood, and cognitive experience is substantial, phase-sensitive, and insufficiently addressed by existing behavioral health tools.

Mission

Evidence-based.
Individual-first.
Not pink and fluffy.

Women's health has a wellness problem. The market for cycle apps, hormonal supplements, and "femtech" is growing rapidly — but the signal-to-noise ratio is low. Aesthetic branding has outpaced clinical evidence. Products are marketed on social media virality rather than randomized controlled trial data. The dominant palette is pink. The dominant claim is empowerment, undefined.

Rhythm is a deliberate alternative. Every feature is grounded in peer-reviewed literature. Every intervention is selected based on effect size and population relevance. The design is clean because the content is rigorous. This is not a wellness brand. It is a clinical decision-support framework for individual neuropsychological health, delivered at consumer scale.

The goal is not to make women feel good about their cycle. The goal is to give them the tools to understand their own neuroscience and act on it — intelligently, consistently, and without mystification.
